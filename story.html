My Journey into Making Pasta

Early Pasta Memories

I've enjoyed pasta since I was a child, whether it's simple spaghetti, angel hair, ravioli, or rotini.
Much of my pasta experience has come from buying boxes of dry pasta and then buying fresh pasta at the grocery or in restaurants.

A Pandemic Shift

My pasta experience changed during the COVID‑19 pandemic. Working from home inspired me to cook more—and to discover that restaurant‑style dishes are possible in a home kitchen.

Baking, Fermenting, and Gearing Up

My first venture was baking sourdough bread with a starter I made myself. That led me to sprout beans, blend sauces, and brew ginger beer. I began buying fewer ready‑made items and more natural ingredients such as meats, vegetables, and grains.

COVID also nudged me to upgrade my equipment. I bought a KitchenAid mixer for kneading dough. Its attachments were a bonus; the five‑piece Pasta Deluxe Set—with a roller and four cutters—was the one that really intrigued me.

The Big Purchase

On February 5th, 2023 I finally bought the set. I was eager to make my own pasta but had no idea what the process entailed.

First Attempts at Pasta Dough

I researched pasta dough recipes online and stuck to my goal of using fresh ingredients. Many call for 100 g of flour per large egg. That seemed simple enough, but when I mixed the dough in my KitchenAid it turned out far stickier than the guides suggested.

Chasing the Perfect Ratio

With a research mindset, I looked for a more precise formula. In bread baking we discuss hydration: 100 g of flour with 50 g of water is 50% hydration. I guessed there must be a similar ratio for pasta.

Deep in a forum thread I found a claim that weighing the egg yolks and multiplying by 1.61 would give the right amount of flour.

After plenty of testing I rounded to 1.6 × (yolk weight). Since adopting that ratio my dough rolls and cuts beautifully—even without the mixer.
